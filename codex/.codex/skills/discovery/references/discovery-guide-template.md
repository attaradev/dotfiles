# Discovery Interview Guide Template

## Interview structure

A 45–60 minute interview follows this arc:

| Phase | Duration | Purpose |
|-------|----------|---------|
| Intro & warm-up | 5 min | Build rapport, set expectations, get consent to record |
| Context setting | 10 min | Understand who they are and their current workflow |
| Core exploration | 25–35 min | Dig into the problem area, past behaviours, and pain points |
| Concept reaction (optional) | 5–10 min | Show a prototype or concept only if hypothesis is already formed |
| Wrap-up | 5 min | Ask what they wish you had asked; thank them |

### Screener criteria

List the criteria a participant must meet to qualify. Include:
- Role / job title
- Behaviour (e.g., "uses the product at least weekly", "has experienced X in the last 3 months")
- Disqualifiers (e.g., "works at a competitor", "never encountered this workflow")

### Introduction script

> "Thanks for taking the time today. I'm [name] and I work on [product/team]. We're doing research to better understand [broad topic — don't reveal the hypothesis]. There are no right or wrong answers — we're here to learn from your experience, not test you. I may take notes or record this session for internal use; is that okay? Feel free to stop me at any time."

### Warm-up questions (5 min)

Open with easy, factual questions to build rapport and understand context.

- Tell me a bit about your role and what a typical day looks like.
- How long have you been doing [relevant activity]?
- What tools do you use most for [relevant workflow]?

### Context-setting questions (10 min)

Understand the user's current state before probing the problem.

- Walk me through the last time you [did the relevant activity]. What triggered it?
- What does the end-to-end process look like for you?
- Who else is involved when you do this?

### Core exploration questions (25–35 min)

These are the most important questions. Each should probe a specific assumption.

**Assumption being tested:** [State it explicitly in the guide so the interviewer knows what evidence to listen for]

Questions:
- Tell me about the last time you struggled with [problem area]. What happened?
- What did you do to work around it?
- How often does that happen?
- What was the impact when it went wrong?
- What have you tried in the past to fix it?
- If you could change one thing about how you currently do [task], what would it be?

**Follow-up probes (use these to go deeper on any answer):**
- Tell me more about that.
- Why was that important to you?
- What did you do next?
- How did that make you feel?
- What would have made that easier?

### Concept reaction (optional, evaluative research only)

Only include if you have a specific prototype or concept to test. Do not show concepts in pure exploratory research — it anchors the conversation.

- Show the concept. "What is your first reaction to this?"
- "What would you expect to happen if you did X?"
- "When would you use this? When would you not?"
- "What is missing? What would make this more useful?"

### Wrap-up

- Is there anything you expected me to ask about that I didn't?
- Is there anyone else I should talk to who would give a different perspective?
- Would you be open to a follow-up conversation?

## Question writing rules

### Do
- Ask about past behaviour: "Tell me about the last time..."
- Ask about frequency and recency: "How often does this happen? When did it last happen?"
- Ask about impact: "What was the consequence when this went wrong?"
- Ask about workarounds: "What did you do instead?"
- Follow surprising answers with silence — let them fill it

### Do not
- Ask hypothetical preference questions: "Would you use a feature that...?" → users say yes to everything hypothetical
- Ask leading questions: "Don't you find it frustrating when...?"
- Confirm your hypothesis: "So you agree that the current process is broken?"
- Offer solutions mid-interview: stay in listen mode until wrap-up
- Ask two questions at once

## Synthesis notes template

After each interview, capture:
- **Surprising quotes:** [Exact words that challenged your assumptions]
- **Patterns:** [Themes that appeared across multiple answers]
- **Behaviours observed:** [What they actually do, not what they say they do]
- **Open questions:** [What you still need to learn]
- **Hypothesis update:** [Did this session strengthen or weaken your hypothesis?]
