# Product Positioning Guide

## Positioning statement structure

```
For [ICP — specific, not "businesses"],
[Product name] is the [category you compete in]
that [primary benefit — measurable outcome if possible]
because [proof point — what makes the benefit credible].

Unlike [primary alternative],
[product name] [key differentiator].
```

Example:
```
For growth-stage SaaS companies managing their first CS team,
Gainsight Lite is the customer health platform
that cuts at-risk churn by 30% before it shows up in MRR
because it surfaces product usage signals, not just ticket counts.

Unlike Salesforce, it requires no admin and is live in a day.
```

---

## Category design

The category you claim determines:
- Who you compete against
- What the buyer compares you to
- What price they expect to pay

Options:

| Strategy | When to use | Risk |
|----------|------------|------|
| **Join existing category** | Category is well-understood, buyers are already looking | Compete on features; price compression |
| **Reframe existing category** | Current category has a flaw you solve | Buyers must accept the reframe |
| **Create new category** | Nothing like you exists yet | Expensive to educate the market |

Most early-stage SaaS should join or reframe an existing category. Creating a new category requires 3–5 years of sustained market education and significant capital.

## Differentiation types

Differentiation is only real if your three closest competitors cannot say the same thing.

| Type | Example | How to validate |
|------|---------|----------------|
| **Feature** | "Only platform with real-time sync" | Is the feature patentable or genuinely unavailable elsewhere? |
| **Speed / ease** | "Live in 1 day, not 3 months" | Benchmark against the alternative's documented onboarding time |
| **Integration** | "Native Salesforce sync — no middleware" | Does the competitor require a Zapier layer? |
| **Vertical focus** | "Built for healthcare billing, not generic invoicing" | Do competitors serve the vertical or is it generic? |
| **Business model** | "Pay per outcome, not per seat" | Is the pricing model genuinely different? |
| **Data / network** | "Trained on 10M support tickets from SaaS companies" | Is the dataset proprietary? |

Avoid these generic claims — they are not differentiation:
- "Easy to use"
- "All-in-one"
- "Powerful and flexible"
- "Enterprise-grade"
- "Best-in-class"

## Message hierarchy

```
Headline          ← One sentence. The ICP + outcome. What you do and for whom.
                    Test: would a competitor say this? If yes, rewrite.

Subheadline       ← Expands the how. The mechanism or key differentiator.
                    One to two sentences.

Value statement 1 ← First supporting benefit with proof point
Value statement 2 ← Second supporting benefit with proof point
Value statement 3 ← Third supporting benefit with proof point

CTA               ← Action that matches acquisition motion
                    (Start free trial / Book a demo / See pricing)
```

### Headline formula options

**Outcome headline**: "Cut support ticket volume by 40% without adding headcount"

**ICP + verb headline**: "The revenue platform for bootstrapped SaaS founders"

**Comparison headline**: "Customer health scores without the Gainsight price tag"

**Problem headline**: "Your best customers are about to churn. You just don't know which ones."

---

## Proof point formats

Every claim must have a corresponding proof point. Use the most specific evidence available:

| Evidence type | Example |
|---------------|---------|
| **Customer metric** | "Acme Corp reduced onboarding time from 3 weeks to 2 days" |
| **Aggregate data** | "Our customers see 23% lower churn in the first 90 days" |
| **Benchmark** | "Setup takes 1 day vs the industry average of 6 weeks" |
| **Capability** | "Native Salesforce integration — no middleware required" |
| **Social proof** | "Trusted by 400+ SaaS companies" (only if the number is meaningful) |

Vague proof points: "Our customers love us" / "Used by companies of all sizes" — omit these.

---

## What to avoid saying

These phrases appear on the website of nearly every SaaS product and signal undifferentiated positioning:

- "The all-in-one platform for X"
- "Simple, powerful, and flexible"
- "Built for teams of all sizes"
- "Streamline your workflow"
- "Take your X to the next level"
- "The future of X"
- "X made easy"

---

## The "same as everyone else" test

Read your headline and ask: could Competitor A, B, or C replace your product name and publish this on their homepage without lying?

If yes: you are not positioned. The headline describes a category, not a product.

Fix: get more specific about:
1. Who exactly it is for (ICP, not "businesses")
2. What outcome (measured, not vague)
3. Why you (mechanism, not aspiration)

---

## Positioning → channel alignment

Positioning must match where the ICP looks for solutions:

| ICP | Where they look | Message format |
|-----|----------------|---------------|
| Developers | Hacker News, GitHub, docs | Technical, show-don't-tell, no jargon |
| Founders | Twitter/X, podcasts, communities | Peer voice, ROI-focused, brief |
| VP/Director | G2, analyst reports, peer referrals | Business outcome, social proof, comparison tables |
| Enterprise | RFP, analyst reports, reference calls | Compliance, security, SLA, total cost of ownership |
